"

Cold emailing is an essential professional skill that everyone should learn. I love the saying, "You miss 100% of the shots you don't take," and it applies perfectly here. What surprised me most was how willing professionals, especially Rice alums, are to speak with undergraduates—especially when you're working on something interesting.

During the cold email exercise in BUSI 221, I connected with a few Rice graduates and learned about job opportunities I didn't even know existed. After the course, my team used cold emailing to reach out to surgeons for an engineering project, and we got some really great responses and feedback.

We even had the chance to observe a kidney transplant surgery at Houston Methodist, explore medical engineering technologies, and are currently in process of bringing on a transplant surgeon as our medical advisor. Cold emailing might not seem immediately impactful during the course, but it's a skill you'll use often and find highly effective in the long run.

"

I started off the cold email grind trying to send as many as possible, but I quickly realized that I was not going to get the responses I needed in time. Instead, I focused on reaching out to people I either had a mutual connection with or shared something in common.

Since I've been using a Boosted Board (electric skateboard) for years, I decided to email the founder, sharing how the product had impacted my daily life and asking for advice on breaking into his industry. To my surprise, he responded, appreciated my enthusiasm, and agreed to get on a call.

After an insightful conversation, he offered to be a resource to me in the future and gave me his personal contact information. With so many cold emails flooding inboxes, the key is to write with genuine intent—reach out to people with whom you have a real connection or a compelling reason to start a conversation.

"

One of the most surprising outcomes from cold emailing was receiving a response from Martín Casado and having the opportunity to speak with him. Casado is a General Partner at Andreessen Horowitz and a pioneer in software-defined networking. I had recently watched an interview he conducted with Mira Murati, who was the CTO of OpenAI. That conversation made me curious about his perspective on building and scaling technical products.

As someone interested in both computer science and entrepreneurship, I wanted to understand how technical founders navigate the transition from building innovative products to creating successful companies. One key takeaway from our conversation was the importance of timing in startups and solving real business problems. He explained that even the best ideas can struggle if the market isn't ready for them, while sometimes a simple idea succeeds because external conditions align at the right moment.

So it's not just about having a great product, but also about recognizing the right go-to-market strategy, and in some cases, the right timing. I also remember discussing his routine and his emphasis on deep focus and structure. Casado spoke about how he values structuring his days to maximize focus and eliminate distractions, which allows him to engage with complex problems.

The most difficult part of cold emailing is often getting a response, so it's essential to craft a message that stands out. Despite this challenge, it can lead to valuable conversations, new insights, and unexpected opportunities.

Key Insights

People are surprisingly willing to help

Many Disco-ers were amazed by how willing professionals were to respond and share their knowledge, especially when approached with genuine interest.

Cold emailing is a numbers game

Disco-ers who sent more emails received more responses. Persistence pays off, but quality and personalization matter too.

Snowball networking effect

One successful connection often leads to more. Many Disco-ers found that their initial contacts would refer them to other valuable connections.

Career-changing opportunities

Cold emails led to unexpected opportunities like job offers, industry insights, and even unique experiences like observing surgeries or meeting industry leaders.

Authenticity matters

Genuine interest and personal connections make cold emails more effective. Finding common ground or shared interests increases response rates.

Lifelong valuable skill

Disco-ers continue to use cold emailing skills long after the course ends, applying them to research, business ventures, and other academic pursuits.
