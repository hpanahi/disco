Why This Guide?

Understanding Possibility Statements

In Disco, you're exploring two possibility statements - guesses about potential futures you might pursue. A possibility statement has three parts:

"I will provide my ability to ________ (the what - what value you'll provide) so that I can help ________ (the who - the people you want to help) because ________ (the why - the reason you want to help and/or the outcome you want them to achieve)"

Your Two Possibility Statements:

  • ðŸŽ“ Most Feasible: The one you would choose if you had to decide tomorrow. The most sensible and possible path.
  • ðŸš€ Most Revolutionary: The one that makes your heart sing - maybe a hobby or passion you don't know how to turn into a future. Exciting, even if it seems unattainable.

These are starting points, not final decisions. You may not even know if these possibilities are possible! That's okay - you'll do research to find out.

The Numbers Game: Why You Need to Contact Many People

You need 4 high-quality, 30-minute+ conversations - these are conversations with people who can give you specific, ungoogleable insights about whether your possibility statement is grounded in reality.

To get 4 quality conversations, you'll likely need to contact around 50 people.

Cold emailing is a numbers game. Not everyone will respond. Some will be too busy. Some won't be the right fit once you explore further. That's completely normal and not personal. To generate the luck you're looking for, you need to reach out to LOTS of people.

This guide shows you how to use AI tools to research people deeply and efficiently - because when you're contacting 50 people, you need a system that scales.

Remember that coffee chats aren't about asking people to hire you or validate your idea. They're about testing your possibility statement through real conversations. The better your research, the better your emails, the higher your response rate - but you still need volume.

Part 1: Finding People to Research

Start with LinkedIn and Your Network

Start with the basics:

  1. LinkedIn search: Look for job titles, companies, and industries related to your possibility statement
  2. Rice connections: Filter for Rice alumni (they're more likely to respond)
  3. Your network: Ask professors, TAs, friends, and family for introductions
  4. Pitchbook: Research companies in your space using Pitchbook, a tool that allows you to to do research on startups and private companies. Youc can sign up for an account for free using your Rice email
  5. Cast a wide net: Your initial list should be 50+ people, not 4-5

Important:

Don't stop at 5-10 people. You need a large pipeline because:

  • Not everyone will respond (expect 10-20% response rate)
  • Some people won't be available for weeks
  • Some will respond but won't be the right fit
  • You need 4 quality conversations, which means contacting ~50 people

Goal: Create an initial list of 50+ potential contacts. Use your Contact Tracking spreadsheet to manage this list and track outreach.

Part 2: Researching People Deeply (The Core Work)

This is where AI with web search really shines (ChatGPT Plus or Gemini work best here). Once you have names from LinkedIn, use these prompts to research each person thoroughly.

Efficiency tip: Use a Tiered Approach

You don't need to research all 50 people at the same depth:

  • Tier 1 (Priority contacts - ~15 people): Deep research using all prompts below
  • Tier 2 (Good fits - ~20 people): Initial discovery + quick validation
  • Tier 3 (Backup options - ~15 people): Basic LinkedIn research only

Start with Tier 1, send those emails, then move to Tier 2 while waiting for responses.

Note for Claude Users

Since Claude doesn't have web search, you'll need to gather information manually first (from LinkedIn, Google, company websites) and then paste it into Claude for analysis. The prompts below still work - just remove "Please search the web" and instead provide Claude with the information you've found.

Stage 3: Synthesis (Prepare for the Email)

After researching 2-3 people, use this prompt to create a summary:

Copied!
I've researched [Person's Name] and I'm ready to write a cold email.

Create a one-page research summary that includes:

1. **Quick Facts**: Current role, company, key expertise
2. **Recent Focus**: What they're working on RIGHT NOW (last 3-6 months)
3. **Connection Points**: 2-3 specific things I should mention that show I
   understand their work
4. **Conversation Starters**: 2-3 recent things they did (articles, talks,
   projects) that I could reference
5. **Best Approach**: Based on their communication style and public presence,
   what tone should I use in my email? (Formal? Casual? Specific question-focused?)

Make this actionable - I should be able to use this summary to write a
personalized cold email in 10 minutes.

Part 3: From Research to Cold Email

Using Your Research Effectively

Your AI research should inform THREE parts of your cold email:

  1. The opening - Show you know who they are
  2. The connection - Explain why you're reaching out to them specifically
  3. The ask - Make it relevant to their expertise

Research Checklist

Building Your Contact List (The Foundation)

  • I've organized them into Tier 1 (15 priority), Tier 2 (20 good fits), Tier 3 (15 backups)
  • I've verified they're currently in the role LinkedIn says
  • I've checked appropriate seniority (not too senior, not too junior)
  • I've prioritized Rice connections where possible
  • I've set aside time over the next 3 weeks to research, email, follow up, and book conversations

Researching Deeply (For Your Tier 1 Contacts)

  • I've used AI to find information beyond their LinkedIn profile
  • I've identified 2-3 recent activities (last 6 months) I can reference
  • I've found at least 1-2 connection points between their work and my possibility statement
  • I understand why THIS person specifically can help me
  • I'm doing lighter research for Tier 2/3 contacts to maintain momentum

Validation

  • This person is actively working in an area relevant to my possibility statement
  • They've shown they're open to sharing their experience (speaking, writing, mentoring)
  • There are no red flags (just changed jobs, on leave, publicly overwhelmed)
  • I have a SPECIFIC question or topic that makes sense to discuss with them

Ready to Email

  • I can explain in 1-2 sentences why I'm reaching out to THEM
  • I have specific, recent work of theirs to reference
  • I know what tone/approach to use based on their style
  • I've drafted 2-3 ungoogleable questions for the coffee chat

Advanced Research Techniques

Cross-Reference Recent Activity

Copied!
I found that [Person's Name] recently [did X thing - gave a talk,
published an article, launched a project, changed jobs].

Search for more context:
- Why did they do this?
- Who else was involved?
- What problem were they solving?
- What was the response or outcome?
- Has there been any follow-up or related work since then?

This will help me ask more informed questions in my coffee chat.

Compare Multiple People

Copied!
I'm deciding between contacting these 3 people for my coffee chat:

1. [Person A Name] - [Job Title] at [Company]
2. [Person B Name] - [Job Title] at [Company]
3. [Person C Name] - [Job Title] at [Company]

My possibility statement: [Brief WHO/WHAT/WHY]

Based on what you can find about each person:
- Who would be MOST relevant to my specific questions?
- Who seems most accessible/likely to respond to a student?
- Who has the most recent, relevant experience?

Rank them and explain your reasoning.

Find Conversation Topics

Copied!
I'm preparing for a coffee chat with [Person's Name].
Based on your research:

1. What are 3 SPECIFIC questions I could ask that show I
   understand their work? (Not generic questions like
   "How did you get into this field?" but questions that
   reference their actual projects or experiences)

2. What's ONE thing they're clearly passionate about that I
   should make sure to ask about?

3. Are there any topics I should avoid? (Recent controversies,
   failed projects, sensitive company information)

Make the questions open-ended and focused on their personal
experience.

Troubleshooting

Problem: "AI can't find much information about this person"

Copied!
I'm trying to research [Person Name] but there's limited public information.

Can you:
1. Verify their current role at [Company]
2. Search for any company announcements, press releases, or team pages
   mentioning them
3. Check if they use a different name professionally or have a maiden name
4. Look for any alumni profiles or university mentions
5. Search for their company's recent projects - they might have been involved

If you truly can't find information, should I contact them anyway, or look
for someone with more public presence?
