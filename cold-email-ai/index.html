Why This Guide?

Understanding Possibility Statements

In Disco, you're exploring two possibility statements - guesses about potential futures you might pursue. A possibility statement has three parts:

"I will provide my ability to ________ (the what - what value you'll provide) so that I can help ________ (the who - the people you want to help) because ________ (the why - the reason you want to help and/or the outcome you want them to achieve)"

Your Two Possibility Statements:

  • ðŸŽ“ Most Feasible: The one you would choose if you had to decide tomorrow. The most sensible and possible path.
  • ðŸš€ Most Revolutionary: The one that makes your heart sing - maybe a hobby or passion you don't know how to turn into a future. Exciting, even if it seems unattainable.

To explore these possibility statements, you need 4 high-quality, 30-minute+ conversations - these are conversations with people who can give you specific, ungoogleable insights about whether your possibility statement is grounded in reality.

To get 4 quality conversations, you'll likely need to contact around 50 people.

Cold emailing is a numbers game. Not everyone will respond. Some will be too busy. Some won't be the right fit once you explore further. That's completely normal and not personal. To generate the luck you're looking for, you need to reach out to LOTS of people.

This guide shows you how to use AI tools to research people deeply and efficiently - because when you're contacting 50 people, you need a system that scales.

Remember that coffee chats aren't about asking people to hire you or validate your idea. They're about testing your possibility statement through real conversations. The better your research, the better your emails, the higher your response rate - but you still need volume.

Use this guide:

  • AFTER you've drafted your possibility statement
  • BEFORE you write your cold email
  • ALONGSIDE the Coffee Chat Guide and How to Prepare for Your Chats

Advanced: Start With YOUR Background (Find Connection-Rich Contacts)

Why This Approach Works

Instead of finding people first and then looking for common ground, flip the process: use YOUR background to systematically identify people who might have mutual connections and/or interests with you

Option 1: Analyze Your Background for Connection Points

Use this prompt to identify all the potential connections you could leverage. Instead of including all your background information in the prompt, you could also attach your resume:

A possibility statement follows this format:
"I will provide my ability to ________ (the what) so that I can help ________ (the who) because ________ (the why)"

I'm building a list of people to contact for coffee chats about my possibility statement.
I want to prioritize people who are closer connections with me for higher response rates.

Here's my background:

EDUCATION:
- Current: [University], [Major], Class of [Year]
- High School: [School Name], [City], [State/Country]
- Study abroad / exchange programs: [if any]

- Internships: [Company, role, dates]
- Projects: [Notable projects or research]
- Part-time work: [Any relevant work]

LOCATION HISTORY:
- Hometown: [City, State/Country]
- Places I've lived: [Cities where you've spent significant time]

INTERESTS & ACTIVITIES:
- Sports/hobbies: [specific activities you do regularly]

- Technical skills: [programming languages, tools, etc.]
- Soft skills: [areas where you have real experience]

WHO: [who you want to help]

2. Suggest types of people who might share these connections

4. Tell me which connections to lead with in cold emails

Focus on authentic connections - things that would genuinely make someone more likely
to respond to a Rice student.

Option 2: Generate Custom LinkedIn Searches with AI

Use this prompt to create 15-20 targeted search queries based on YOUR specific background:

Copied!
I'm a Rice University student exploring a possibility statement. This is a guess about a future I might pursue - I'm not sure yet if it's even possible, but I want to research it.

A possibility statement follows this format:
"I will provide my ability to ________ (the what) so that I can help ________ (the who) because ________ (the why)"

I'm building a list of people to contact for coffee chats about my possibility statement.

I need 15-20 LinkedIn search queries that will help me find people who:
1. Are relevant to my possibility statement
2. Share connections with me (for higher response rates)

My background:
- School: [Your University], [Major], Class of [Year]
- High School: [School Name] in [City, State]
- Hometown: [City]
- Interests: [3-5 specific interests/hobbies]
- Experience: [Key internships, projects, or skills]

My possibility statement:
WHO: [who you want to help]
WHAT: [skills you want to provide]
WHY: [outcomes you want to create]

For each search query, provide:
1. The exact LinkedIn search string (using boolean operators: AND, OR, NOT, quotes)
2. Which connection point it leverages (e.g., "Rice alumni", "Houston hometown")
3. Why this search would find relevant people for my possibility statement
4. Approximate seniority level to expect

Organize the searches in priority order:
- Tier 1 (strongest connections): 5 searches
- Tier 2 (good connections): 5 searches
- Tier 3 (relevant but no personal connection): 5 searches

Make the searches specific enough to return 50-200 results each, not thousands.

Pro Tips for Connection-Rich Outreach

  • Save your searches: LinkedIn allows you to save searches and get weekly alerts for new people matching your criteria
  • Use multiple connection points: If someone is both a Rice alum AND from your hometown, mention both!
  • Be authentic: Don't force connections that aren't real. "We both went to Rice" is better than stretching to create false common ground
  • Track what works: Note which connection types get the highest response rates in your tracking spreadsheet
  • Don't ignore weak ties: Even "we both like rock climbing" can be a conversation starter if genuine

Part 2: Researching People Deeply (The Core Work)

This is where AI with web search really shines (ChatGPT Plus or Gemini work best here). Once you have names from LinkedIn, use these prompts to research each person thoroughly.

Efficiency tip: Use a Tiered Approach

You don't need to research all 50 people at the same depth:

  • Tier 1 (Priority contacts - ~15 people): Deep research using all prompts below
  • Tier 2 (Good fits - ~20 people): Initial discovery + quick validation
  • Tier 3 (Backup options - ~15 people): Basic LinkedIn research only

Start with Tier 1, send those emails, then move to Tier 2 while waiting for responses.

Stage 3: Synthesis (Prepare for the Email)

After researching 2-3 people, use this prompt to create a summary:

Copied!
I've researched [Person's Name] and I'm ready to write a cold email.

Create a one-page research summary that includes:

2. **Recent Focus**: What they're working on RIGHT NOW (last 3-6 months)
3. **Connection Points**: 2-3 specific things I should mention that show I

5. **Best Approach**: Based on their communication style and public presence,

Make this actionable - I should be able to use this summary to write a
personalized cold email in 10 minutes.

Part 3: From Research to Cold Email

Using Your Research Effectively

Your AI research should inform THREE parts of your cold email:

  1. The opening - Show you know who they are
  2. The connection - Explain why you're reaching out to them specifically
  3. The ask - Make it relevant to their expertise

What AI Found:

  • She transitioned from consulting to product management 5 years ago

Compare Multiple People

- Who would be MOST relevant to my specific questions?
- Who seems most accessible/likely to respond to a student?

Find Conversation Topics

Copied!

Based on your research:

1. What are 3 SPECIFIC questions I could ask that show I

   "How did you get into this field?" but questions that

Copied!

1. Verify their current role at [Company]
2. Search for any company announcements, press releases, or team pages
   mentioning them

4. Look for any alumni profiles or university mentions
5. Search for their company's recent projects - they might have been involved

Ethical Considerations

  • Be transparent that you've done research (that's a GOOD thing - shows you value their time)
  • Don't claim connections that don't exist
  • Don't mention personal information that wasn't publicly shared
  • Don't use AI-generated content without fact-checking it first
  • If AI can't find information, don't make it up - reach out to someone else or do more manual research

Additional Resources

After using AI for research, don't forget to:

  1. Track your outreach: Use the Contact Tracking spreadsheet
  2. Prepare questions: Use the "How to Prepare for Your Chats" guide
  3. Write your email: Use the example in the "Coffee Chat Guide"

Remember: Cold emailing is a numbers game. If you don't get a response, it's not personal. To get 4 quality conversations, you'll need to contact around 50 people. This is about volume AND personalization - use AI to research efficiently so you can reach more people with better emails. Keep researching, keep reaching out, and keep refining your approach.

Timeline for Your Outreach

Over the next three weeks:

  • Week 2: Research and email Tier 2 contacts, follow up with Tier 1, start booking conversations
